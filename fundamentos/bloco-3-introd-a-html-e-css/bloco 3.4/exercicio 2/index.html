technical stuff:

Odontodactylus scyllarus, or mantis shrimp, are carnivorous marine crustaceans of the order Stomatopoda, branching from other members
of the class Malacostraca around 340 million years ago. The Mantis shrimp typically grow to around 10 cm (3.9 in) in length,
while a few can reach up to 38 cm (15 in), they belong to the arthropoda phylum and crustacea sub-phylum. They belong to the animalia kingdom, in case you didn't know.

About 450 species of mantis shrimp have been discovered worldwide; all living species are in the suborder Unipeltata, which arose around 193 million years ago.

... Called "sea locusts" by ancient Assyrians, "prawn killers" in Australia,[5] and now sometimes referred to as "thumb splitters"—because of the animal's ability to inflict painful wounds if handled incautiously[6]—mantis shrimp have powerful raptorials that are used to attack and kill prey either by spearing, stunning, or dismembering. Some mantis shrimp species have specialised calcified "clubs" that can strike with great power, while others have sharp forelimbs used to seize the prey (hence the term "mantis" in its common name).

where do they live?

The mantis shrimp lives in warm, shallow water, and typically grows to be between 6 - 12 inches (15, 30 cm) in lenght

want to have a pet Mantis shrimp? read this first

Aquariums don't typically house mantis shrimps because they tend to slaughter every other creature they share a tank with.

they have very fast movements

Their limbs move so quickly the water around them boils in a process know as supercavitation. When these cavitation bubbles
collapse, it produces an undersea shockwave that can kill prey even if the mantis shrimp misses its target.
